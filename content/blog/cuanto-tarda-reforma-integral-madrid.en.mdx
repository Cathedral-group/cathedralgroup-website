Planning a full renovation of a high-end property in Madrid means understanding the timelines honestly. The question "how long does it take?" has no single answer, because the schedule depends on the condition of the property, the level of finish and, above all, the administrative process. In this guide we break down the real timelines phase by phase, based on market benchmarks, so you can plan ahead and make decisions with peace of mind.

## How long does a full renovation take in Madrid?

As a market benchmark, a [full renovation in Madrid](/servicios/reformas-integrales-madrid) is usually completed within a typical range of **3 to 6 months** of on-site works. On high-end projects using imported materials (selected natural stone, bespoke joinery, ironmongery and finishes sourced internationally with long lead times) the period can extend to as much as **8 months**.

It is worth distinguishing between two clocks that run in parallel, though not always at the same time:

- **The administrative clock**: processing the building permit with Madrid City Council.
- **The works clock**: the physical execution, from demolition through to the finishes.

### What makes a renovation take longer

The overall timeline moves within the range above depending on several factors:

| Factor | Effect on the timeline |
|--------|----------------------|
| Condition of the services | Replacing the plumbing, electrics and climate control in full lengthens the technical phase |
| Type of permit | Major works (obra mayor) involve a longer process than notified works |
| Imported materials | International lead times can set the pace of the works |
| Listed or historic building | Additional requirements and possibly specific reports |
| Quality of the prior project | A well-defined project avoids stoppages and changes on site |

The conclusion is clear: the timeline is set not by the works team alone, but by the sum of project, permit, supplies and execution. Making decisions early is the best way to keep to the schedule.

## The phases of a full renovation, one by one

A well-managed full renovation progresses through a chain of linked phases. Each one carries its own weight in the schedule and its own technical logic. These are the six main stages, in order:

### 1. Project and permit

This is the most underestimated phase, and the one that most determines when the works actually begin. It includes the survey of the existing condition, the layout design, the selection of materials, the renders and the technical documentation needed to apply for the permit.

The **major works permit (obra mayor) is typically processed within a range of 2 to 4 months** at Madrid City Council, depending on market benchmarks and the complexity of the application. Not every intervention requires a major works permit: where the structure, façade and essential configuration of the property are not affected, a more streamlined process may suffice. To understand which regime applies in your case, we recommend our [guide to the building permit in Madrid](/blog/licencia-obra-madrid-guia).

> Recommendation: the project should be fully finalised before applying for the permit. Any subsequent change can mean reworking the documentation and restarting the timeline.

### 2. Demolition and removal of debris

Once the permit is granted, the physical works begin. The demolition phase clears the space: partition walls, old finishes, floors and obsolete services are removed, and the debris is managed in line with waste regulations. It is a relatively quick stage, but a critical one, because opening up the property is when the surprises of older buildings come to light (damp, services that no longer meet code, structure in need of reinforcement).

### 3. Building services

Once the property has been stripped out, the services are installed: plumbing and drainage, electrics in line with the Reglamento Electrotécnico de Baja Tensión (REBT, the Low Voltage Electrotechnical Regulation), climate control (air-source heat pump or ducted systems) and, on high-end projects, home automation and the first-fix wiring for architectural lighting. This is one of the most technically demanding phases and the foundation on which everything else is built.

### 4. Building work and finishes

With the services under control comes the closing-up of the property: new partition walls following the designed layout, suspended ceilings, boxing-in of the services, wall tiling and floor laying. This phase gives the spaces their final form and is usually the one with the most sustained team presence on site.

### 5. Kitchen, bathrooms and joinery

This is the phase where the project starts to look finished. The kitchen and bathroom units, sanitaryware and taps are fitted, and the internal joinery is installed (doors, bespoke wardrobes, fronts). This is where imported materials weigh most heavily: if a one-off piece has a long lead time, it conditions the entire schedule. Planning the orders well in advance during the project phase is what prevents unnecessary waits.

### 6. Finishes

The home straight: painting, fitting the lighting, ironmongery, adjustments, site clean-up and snagging. This is the phase that defines how the quality of a high-end property is perceived, where the detail makes the difference. It ends with the final inspection and handover.

## How the phases overlap on the real schedule

On a well-managed project, the phases are not watertight compartments: some overlap to optimise the overall timeline. While the building work progresses in one area, the services can be going in elsewhere; while the finishes are being carried out in the common areas, the kitchen and bathrooms are being fitted.

As a rough guide, this is how the weight of each stage is distributed within the works period:

| Phase | Approximate weight in the works schedule |
|------|------------------------------------------|
| Demolition | Short |
| Building services | Medium-high |
| Building work and finishes | High |
| Kitchen, bathrooms and joinery | Medium |
| Finishes | Medium |

Remember that the **project and permit phase comes before** this works schedule and runs separately: that is why, when someone asks "how long does it take?", the honest answer also includes those months of administrative processing.

## Keys to keeping your renovation on schedule

Experience shows that delays rarely come from the works themselves, but from decisions that arrive too late. These are the practices that most help to keep to the schedule:

1. **Finalise the project before applying for the permit.** A project defined down to the detail avoids changes that restart the administrative timeline.
2. **Order imported materials early.** Supplies with long lead times should be ordered as soon as the design is finalised, not when their phase comes round.
3. **Make your selection decisions in good time.** Finishes, taps, joinery and surfaces defined early free the works from waiting.
4. **Allow a margin for the unexpected.** In older buildings it is wise to build in a time buffer for structural or services surprises.
5. **Rely on an integrated studio.** Having one and the same team handle design, permit and execution removes the dead time between different parties and reduces the risk of poor coordination.
