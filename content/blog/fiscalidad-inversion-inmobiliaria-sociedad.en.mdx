## Investing in property: a decision that begins with tax

Before signing the purchase of a property intended for rental income or capital appreciation, it is worth settling a question that shapes the entire return: is it better to acquire it in your own name or through a company? The structure you choose determines which taxes you pay, how the profits are taxed, and what happens further down the line with the sale or succession.

There is no universal answer. The decision depends on the size of your assets, the investment horizon, and whether you expect to reinvest the profits or draw on them. At [Cathedral Capital](/capital) we work alongside investors who structure their property holdings in Madrid, always on one premise: complying with the regulations rigorously and planning with judgement. This guide sets out the reference tax framework to inform that conversation.

## The individual route: taxation under IRPF

When an individual buys a property and lets it out, the income is taxed in the general base of IRPF (personal income tax), added to the taxpayer's other income (salary, business activities, and so on). The applicable rate is progressive: the higher the total income, the higher the percentage.

### The incentive for letting a main home

IRPF (personal income tax) legislation provides for a **60% reduction on the net income** from a let when the property serves as the tenant's main home. In other words, once the deductible expenses have been subtracted (interest, IBI (council property tax), service charges, insurance, depreciation, repairs), only the remaining 40% is included in the taxable base.

This reduction is one of the main attractions of investing as an individual in residential property. Two caveats are worth highlighting, in line with the regulations in force and their successive reforms:

- The reduction applies to **letting a main home**, not to holiday lets, seasonal lets or commercial premises.
- The percentage and its conditions have been affected by recent reforms aimed at high-pressure housing areas; before applying the 60% it is worth checking the specific case with an adviser.

### When the individual approach fits

Investing in your own name tends to be simpler and more efficient for one or a few properties, where the aim is to hold the rental income for the long term and the asset base is not large. It avoids the costs of setting up and maintaining a corporate structure and lets you take advantage of the 60% reduction on a main home.

## The company route: corporate tax

Contributing or buying the properties through a company changes the tax picture entirely. The profits no longer go through the investor's IRPF (personal income tax); instead they are taxed under **Impuesto de Sociedades (corporate tax)**, whose general rate is **25%**.

### Key differences versus the individual

| Aspect | Individual (IRPF) | Company (IS) |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Taxation of profit | Progressive rate (general base) | General rate 25% |
| Main-home reduction | Yes (60% of net income) | Does not apply |
| Reinvestment of profits | Income already taxed under IRPF | Allows reinvestment before distribution |
| Access to funds | Direct withdrawal | Distribution via dividends (taxed again) |
| Succession | Inheritance of properties | Transfer of shares |

The appeal of a company arises above all when the investor's marginal IRPF (personal income tax) rate comfortably exceeds 25%, and when the profits are earmarked for **reinvestment** in new transactions rather than being spent. As long as the money stays within the company and is reinvested, the second level of taxation does not arise.

### The cost of distribution

It is worth remembering the effect of double taxation: what the company earns is taxed first at 25% and then, when it is distributed as a dividend to the shareholder, it is taxed again in their IRPF (personal income tax) as savings income. That is why a company shines in strategies of accumulation and reinvestment, and loses its advantage when the aim is to draw on the profit straight away.

## Taxes on the purchase: ITP and IVA

Beyond how the income is taxed, the acquisition itself carries a tax burden, which depends on the type of property:

- **Second-hand home (transfer between individuals, or from a company that is not acting as a developer):** the **ITP (transfer tax)** applies, a levy devolved to the autonomous regions. In the Region of Madrid the rate is set by the autonomous legislation in force (BOCM), so it is worth confirming the applicable percentage at the time of the transaction.
- **New build and first delivery by the developer:** the transaction is subject to **IVA (VAT)** (plus stamp duty), not ITP (transfer tax). IVA (VAT) is recoverable for taxable persons who use the property for an economic activity, which in certain transactions tips the balance towards a corporate structure.

This distinction between ITP (transfer tax) and IVA (VAT) is one of the factors that weighs most heavily when deciding between the individual and company routes, especially in new-build transactions, where recovering the IVA (VAT) can be decisive.

## The SOCIMI: a vehicle for large portfolios

For high volumes and a long-term commitment to letting, there is a vehicle with a special tax regime: the **SOCIMI (Spanish REIT)** (Sociedad Anónima Cotizada de Inversión en el Mercado Inmobiliario). It is the Spanish equivalent of the international REITs.

Its essential features, within its specific regime, are:

- A focus on the **acquisition and management of properties for letting**.
- A tax regime of its own, conditional on meeting demanding requirements on capital, stock-market listing and profit distribution.
- The obligation to **distribute a large part of the profits** as dividends, which shifts part of the taxation onto the investor.

The SOCIMI (Spanish REIT) is not a formula for the investor buying one or two flats: it demands a scale, a structure and compliance costs that are only justified in portfolios of a certain size. It is an arena where professional planning is indispensable.

## How to decide: three questions that make the difference

Reduced to its essentials, the choice between individual and company turns on three axes:

1. **Size of the asset base.** The more properties and the higher the total income, the more likely it is that the 25% Impuesto de Sociedades (corporate tax) rate will prove more efficient than the progressive marginal rate of IRPF (personal income tax).
2. **Reinvestment versus withdrawal.** If the strategy is to accumulate and reinvest, a company lets you put the profit to work before being taxed on its distribution. If you want to draw on the money, the individual route avoids double taxation.
3. **Succession and transfer.** Transferring shares in a company can offer a different approach to succession planning than the direct inheritance of properties. It is a long-term variable worth anticipating.

To these axes are added nuances such as the type of property (residential versus commercial), the intended use (steady rental income or turnover, as in [property flipping](/blog/flipping-inmobiliario-madrid)) and the autonomous tax regime of Madrid.

## A necessary caveat

The information in this guide sets out the general reference framework and the usual ranges, but property taxation is sensitive to personal circumstances, to the type of transaction and to regulatory reforms. The figures and percentages mentioned should always be checked against the regulations in force at the time of each transaction and against the official sources (AEAT, BOCM, the autonomous legislation of the Region of Madrid).

**Before setting up a company or signing a purchase, consult a tax adviser.** A poorly chosen structure can turn a good investment into a burden; a well-planned one can legally optimise the return for years.
