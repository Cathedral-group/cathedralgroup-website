## The year of warm minimalism

2026 isn't defined by a single colour or a single piece of furniture, but by a feeling: calm. The international design press agrees on one direction —**Dezeen** sums it up as "curated calm over superficial opulence"— and in Spain it has been named, literally, *warm minimalism*. Less noise, more matter. Less pose, more honesty in the finishes.

For a high-end renovation, this translates into one very concrete decision: **choosing the right materials**. It is the materials —their texture, their feel, how they age— that give character to a serene space. These are the ones defining the year.

## The palette: from cold grey to warm brown

The most visible shift of 2026 is one of temperature. Cold greys and clinical whites give way to **chocolate browns, sand tones and warm greige**. Benjamin Moore has named a deep umber its colour of the year, and the Spanish design press talks about satin browns, soft clays and pale terracottas as the new neutral.

Over that calm base come **jewel accents** —burgundy, olive, plum— in small doses: an armchair, a textile, a single piece. Depth without stridency.

## Six materials that define 2026

### 1. Microcement, lime and plaster

The continuous, seamless mineral wall is the great protagonist. Microcement, lime plaster or tadelakt create handcrafted surfaces that wrap the room and react to natural light. They replace wallpaper and the accent wall.

### 2. Travertine

Having emerged strongly from the Salone del Mobile, travertine is the stone of the year: warm, porous, serene. It works in bathrooms, worktops, benches and cladding, bringing the natural patina that warm minimalism calls for.

### 3. Walnut and warm woods

The wood of 2026 has body. Walnut —and treated, smoked or toasted oaks— bring warmth and depth, away from the pale oak of previous years. Bespoke joinery, panelling and furniture.

### 4. Patinated brass and bronze

The single, glossy finish is over. Now metals are mixed and living finishes are sought —unlacquered brass, bronze, blackened steel— that **age with use**. Patina is no longer a flaw: it is a sign of authenticity.

### 5. Large-format sintered stone

For seamless continuous surfaces —worktops, fronts, floors— large-format sintered stone (Dekton, Silestone, Neolith) offers the monolithic look of the moment with outstanding technical performance. A safe bet, and a Spanish one: Cosentino is from Almería.

### 6. Fluted wood

Fluted or reeded wood brings relief, rhythm and a soft shadow that adds depth to fronts, walls and furniture. Discreet sophistication, used with restraint.
