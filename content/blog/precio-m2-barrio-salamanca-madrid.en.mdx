## How much does a square metre cost in the Salamanca district in 2026?

The Salamanca district remains the most sought-after residential district in Madrid and one of the most expensive in Spain. According to the market indices for the first quarter of 2026, the average price of a finished home stands at around **7.724 €/m²**, a figure that serves as a starting reference but conceals enormous differences depending on the street, the subarea and the condition of the property.

In the most exclusive locations —the prime axes of the Castellana and Recoletos— the typical range comfortably exceeds **12.000 €/m²**, and renovated penthouses in landmark buildings can reach exceptional values of up to **19.000 €/m²**. Understanding this range is essential to avoid overpaying or letting an opportunity slip by. At [Cathedral Properties](/properties) we bring sound judgement to that reading of the market.

## Price by subarea: the five faces of the Salamanca district

The district is not uniform. Each subarea has its own character, its own type of building and, consequently, its own price level. These are the indicative market ranges for finished homes in 2026.

| Subarea | Character | Indicative price (€/m²) |
|---------|----------|---------------------------|
| Recoletos (prime axis) | Maximum exclusivity, stately buildings | 12.000 - 19.000 |
| Castellana (prime axis) | Prestigious, outward-facing and high up | 12.000 - 17.000 |
| Goya | Established commercial and residential | 7.500 - 11.000 |
| Lista | Quiet, family-oriented residential | 7.000 - 9.500 |
| Fuente del Berro | Greener and more secluded, better value | 6.500 - 8.500 |

As a reference, the district average (~7.724 €/m²) is driven above all by Goya, Lista and Fuente del Berro, while Recoletos and Castellana pull the top values up. The same budget buys very different realities depending on whether or not you cross the invisible border that separates the prime axis from the rest.

### Recoletos and Castellana: the prime axis

These are the most coveted addresses in Madrid. Buildings dating from the early twentieth century, high ceilings, entrance halls with a concierge and a national and international demand that keeps prices firm even during adjustment cycles. Here the price per sqm rarely drops below 12.000 € and, for unique, renovated stock, climbs towards 17.000 - 19.000 €/m².

### Goya, Lista and Fuente del Berro

Goya combines the commercial appeal of the street of the same name with a solid residential stock. Lista offers a more reserved, family atmosphere, much sought after by buyers who prioritise peace and quiet without giving up a central location. Fuente del Berro, beside the park of the same name, tends to offer the most favourable value in the district and appeals to those looking for high-end living with a little more calm and greenery.

## What makes the price vary within the same street

Two flats in the same building can be separated by thousands of euros per square metre. These are the factors that carry the most weight:

- **Condition and renovation.** A home renovated to a high standard can be worth between 20 % and 40 % more per sqm than the same floor area in need of renovation. The experienced investor factors in the cost and disruption of the works; the end buyer pays for the peace of mind of moving straight in.
- **Floor and height.** Upper floors, with more light and better views, command higher prices than ground floors and mezzanines. In stately buildings, the principal floor (the former noble floor) retains a higher symbolic and real value.
- **Aspect and outlook.** An outward-facing property, sunny and on a main street, clearly outperforms an inward-facing one or one overlooking a courtyard. In the Salamanca district, natural light is one of the variables that most moves the price.
- **Uniqueness of the property.** Penthouses with a terrace, duplexes, homes with original protected features (mouldings, hydraulic floor tiles, period joinery) or with an included parking space reach the top of the range.

### The renovation factor, in detail

When comparing the price of a finished home with that of one in need of renovation, it is worth isolating the cost of the works. A [high-end renovation in the Salamanca district](/zonas/reformas-salamanca) has its own budget, its own timescales and its own impact on the final value. In prime stock, a well-executed renovation not only recovers the investment: it is often what separates a saleable flat from an exceptional one.

## How to read the price if you are going to invest

For an investor, the purchase price is only half of the equation. The other half is what the market is willing to pay afterwards, whether in resale or in a high-end rental. The Salamanca district stands out for its liquidity: demand is deep and constant, which reduces the risk of being left with a stagnant asset.

A few keys to a well-judged purchase:

1. **Buy below the average for its subarea** when the property needs work, and set aside the margin for the renovation.
2. **Prioritise the aspect and the height**: these are the features the market always rewards and that best sustain value.
3. **Check the planning and registry status** before closing: the Catastro, the Land Registry and the Madrid City Council regulations determine what can and cannot be renovated, especially in listed buildings.
4. **Calculate the net yield**, not just the gross, deducting IBI, service charges, tax (AEAT) and management costs.

## Tax and costs associated with the purchase

The price per sqm is not the total cost of acquiring a home. In a resale purchase in the Region of Madrid you have to add Transfer Tax (ITP), governed by the regional legislation published in the BOCM, notary and Registry fees, and professional fees. The property is also subject to recurring taxes such as the municipal IBI.

It is wise to budget for these items from the outset to avoid surprises at completion. Tax planning —always by the correct route and in line with AEAT regulations— is part of any properly conducted deal in this segment.
