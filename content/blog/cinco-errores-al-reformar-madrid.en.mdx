## A renovation is won before the building work starts

Most renovation problems don't appear with a sledgehammer in hand, but long before: in the lack of a project, a poorly closed budget, or a decision made on price alone. The good news is that almost all of them can be avoided. These are the five mistakes that cost the most, and how not to fall for them.

## 1. Starting without a project or a closed budget

Beginning the works without a defined project and a detailed budget is the fastest route to cost overruns. Without a clear scope —what is done, with what finishes and at what price— every decision made on the fly is an unforeseen invoice. A good technical project is not an expense: it is what keeps the budget and the schedule under control.

## 2. Choosing on price alone

The cheapest quote is almost never the most economical. Behind a very low price, the important things are usually missing: site management, guarantee, quality materials or a solvent company that stands behind the work. Compare quotes line by line and be wary of anything that looks too good: the difference is paid later, with interest.

## 3. Ignoring licences and regulations

Carrying out works without the corresponding licence can end in penalties and serious problems when selling or letting. In Madrid, depending on the scope, the intervention is channelled through a planning licence or a self-certified declaration, and you must comply with the Technical Building Code and municipal ordinances. Regulations come first, not last.

## 4. Forgetting what you can't see

What truly makes the difference in a renovation lives behind the wall: plumbing, electrics, climate control, insulation. Renewing finishes over old installations is building on sand. Investing in what you can't see —and doing it right once— avoids 80% of future problems.

## 5. Not planning for the unexpected

In every renovation, especially in an older building, something always comes up: a downpipe in poor condition, a slab that needs reinforcing, hidden damp. Failing to plan a financial cushion or a realistic timeline turns any surprise into a conflict. Setting aside 10-15% for contingencies and working to an honest schedule is a sign of professionalism, not insecurity.
