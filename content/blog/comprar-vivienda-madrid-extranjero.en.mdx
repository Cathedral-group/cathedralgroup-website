Buying a home in Madrid as a foreigner remains perfectly possible in 2026, but the framework has shifted on one important point: the well-known Golden Visa granted for purchasing property has gone. This guide explains what that change means for the international buyer and sets out, step by step, everything you need to acquire a [high-end property in Madrid](/properties) with full legal certainty.

## The end of the Golden Visa: what changes and what doesn't

For a decade, Law 14/2013 allowed anyone investing 500.000 euros or more in Spanish real estate to obtain a residence permit (the so-called Golden Visa). Following the reform approved under Organic Law 1/2025, of 2 January, on measures to improve the efficiency of the Public Justice Service, that route to residence **through property purchase has been abolished and ceased to be in force on 3 April 2025**.

### What this does NOT prevent

It is worth clearing up the main misunderstanding straight away: the end of the Golden Visa **neither prohibits nor complicates the purchase itself**. Any foreign national — resident or non-resident, EU citizen or from outside the EU — can still buy, sell and inherit property in Madrid on equal terms. The only thing that disappears is the automatic link between investing in bricks and mortar and obtaining residence.

### What does change for the international buyer

- The purchase **no longer confers a right of residence in itself**: anyone looking to live in Spain will need to rely on other routes (the remote-working or digital nomad visa, non-lucrative residence, family reunification, and so on), arranged separately from the property transaction.
- The **non-resident buyer** acquiring as an investment or second home retains full capacity to buy, but takes on specific tax obligations that are best planned for in advance (see below).
- **Due diligence and financing** take on greater importance: without the immigration incentive, the decision rests purely on the value and the returns of the asset.

## Step 1: the NIE, your key to entry

The **Foreigner Identification Number (NIE)** is essential. Without it you cannot sign the deed, open a bank account or pay the taxes. You apply for it at the National Police or, if you live outside Spain, at the Spanish consulate in your country. It is advisable to start the process several weeks in advance, as securing an appointment tends to be the main bottleneck in the timetable.

## Step 2: bank account and financing

Although the purchase can be completed by international transfer, opening a **bank account in Spain** makes the transaction far simpler: setting up direct debits for utilities, paying taxes and, where applicable, servicing the mortgage.

A non-resident applying for financing will find the terms somewhat more demanding than a resident: Spanish banks tend to finance a smaller percentage of the valuation and require a larger contribution of the buyer's own funds. ## Step 3: due diligence, the heart of the transaction

This is where the real risk of any purchase is concentrated. Before committing a single euro, you must verify:

### Essential documentation to review

- **Land Registry extract (nota simple)**: confirms who the owner is, the description of the property and, above all, any **charges** (mortgages, attachments, easements, encumbrances).
- **Outstanding charges and debts**: debts owed to the residents' association, unpaid IBI (council tax) bills or approved levies that the buyer could inherit.
- **ITE / IEE**: the Technical Building Inspection (or Building Evaluation Report) certifies the state of repair. Within Madrid's historic building stock this is a critical document.
- **Energy performance certificate** and first-occupancy licence (licencia de primera ocupación).
- **Planning status** and any potential infringements, particularly where earlier renovations were never regularised.

These checks are cross-referenced against official sources such as the **Land Registry**, the **Land Registry Cadastre (Catastro)** and **Madrid City Council**. A professional review of this stage heads off the vast majority of subsequent problems.

## Step 4: purchase taxes and costs

The price of the home is not the total cost. Depending on the applicable regime, the buyer should add a further **10% to 13%** in taxes and costs. The main item is the transfer tax, which depends on whether the home is new or pre-owned.

### New build versus pre-owned

| Item | New build (from a developer) | Pre-owned |
|----------|------------------------------|--------------|
| Main tax | VAT (standard rate for housing) | ITP (Property Transfer Tax) |
| Regulating authority | AEAT (national tax agency) | Region of Madrid (BOCM) |
| AJD (Stamp Duty on Documented Legal Acts) | Applies | Generally does not apply |

### Other costs to budget for

- **Notary**: fees for executing the public deed.
- **Land Registry**: registration of the new ownership.
- **Conveyancing agent (gestoría)**: processing tax filings and registrations (optional but common).
- Independent **legal advice**, strongly recommended for the international buyer.

As a market benchmark, the **usual range of purchase costs sits between 10% and 13% of the price**, varying according to the type of home and the structure of the transaction. It is always worth confirming the prevailing ITP rates in the Region of Madrid and the national VAT rate, as these are updated periodically.

## Step 5: signing, registration and the non-resident's obligations

The transaction is completed before a notary with the **public deed of sale**, at which point the price and the keys are handed over. Afterwards:

1. The **taxes are paid** (ITP or VAT + AJD) within the legal deadlines.
2. The **deed is registered** at the Land Registry in the name of the new owner.
3. The buyer is registered for **IBI (council tax)** and the utility accounts are transferred into their name.

The **non-resident buyer** must also bear in mind their ongoing tax obligations: Non-Resident Income Tax (IRNR), payable simply for owning the property or for letting it out, managed through the **AEAT**, and the possible appointment of a tax representative in Spain. Planning for this from the outset avoids unwelcome surprises in later tax returns.
