## Buying off-plan in Madrid: what it is and why it appeals

Buying off-plan means acquiring a property before it has been built, on the basis of the project, the plans and the specifications set out by the developer. It is one of the most common ways to access [high-end new build in Madrid](/developments), and also one that raises the most questions: is it safe to pay for something that does not yet exist?

The short answer is yes, provided you are well acquainted with the advantages, the risks and, above all, the robust framework of legal guarantees that protects the buyer in Spain. This guide works through all three in a rigorous way so that you can make an informed decision.

## The advantages of buying off-plan

Buying at the project stage offers benefits that are rarely available when purchasing a completed or second-hand property.

### A more competitive entry price

The price during the early sales phases is usually lower than that same property will command once finished and handed over. The developer rewards early confidence, and the increase in value over the construction period largely accrues to the buyer.

### Bespoke finishes

Reserving early allows you to weigh in on decisions that are already fixed in a completed property: the layout of certain spaces, materials, wall and floor finishes, sanitaryware or home automation. In high-end projects, this scope for personalisation is one of the great attractions.

### Phased financing

The outlay is not concentrated in a single moment. It is spread across the build through staged payments up to the handover of keys, when the deed is completed and, where applicable, the mortgage is subrogated or signed. This rhythm makes financial planning easier.

### Efficiency and build quality

New build is delivered under current technical regulations (Código Técnico de la Edificación, the Spanish Technical Building Code), with better performance in insulation, climate control and energy efficiency than housing stock built decades ago. This translates into lower running costs and greater comfort from day one.

| Aspect | Off-plan | Completed property |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|
| Entry price | More competitive in the early phases | Fixed market price |
| Personalisation | High (materials, layout) | Limited or none |
| Method of payment | Phased over the build | Single payment on signing |
| Energy efficiency | More demanding regulations | Depends on year of construction |
| Availability | Deferred until handover | Immediate |

## Risks to bear in mind

Being realistic about the risks is part of a responsible purchase. The two most relevant are timescales and the soundness of the party carrying out the development.

### Delays to handover

Construction is subject to factors that can stretch timescales: municipal permits, supplies, the weather or issues on site. It is therefore worth checking that the contract sets a handover date and the consequences in the event of delay.

### The developer's solvency and track record

The underlying risk is the developer's ability to see the build through to completion. Looking into its track record, its previous developments and its financial backing matters just as much as studying the plans. A sound developer that specialises in [property development projects](/developments) dramatically reduces the uncertainty.

### Differences between what was designed and what is delivered

It is worth insisting on a detailed specification document and keeping it safe. It is the document that lets you confirm, at handover, that what has been built matches what was contracted.

- Check that the contract sets out a handover date and penalties for delay.
- Request and keep the signed specification document.
- Verify the planning status and the building permit for the development.
- Make sure that the amounts you hand over are bonded or insured (see the next section).

## The legal guarantees: your greatest protection

This is the point that turns buying off-plan into a safe transaction. Spanish regulations require the buyer to be protected by several layers of guarantee. It is always advisable to treat these as a general reference and to check each case with legal advice.

### Bond or insurance for sums paid on account

Every amount the buyer hands over before the property is delivered must be guaranteed by means of a bank bond or insurance contract. This obligation is set out in the **Ley 38/1999 de Ordenación de la Edificación** (the Building Regulation Act), as amended by **Ley 20/2015**. If the build is not delivered on the agreed terms, the buyer can recover the sums paid plus the corresponding interest.

In practice, this means that the money you pay over during construction is not left exposed: it is backed by a financial institution or insurer.

### Ten-year structural damage insurance

The developer is required to take out **ten-year insurance** (seguro decenal) that covers, for **ten years**, any material damage affecting the foundations, the supports, the beams, the floor slabs, the load-bearing walls or other structural elements that compromises the strength and stability of the building. It is the underlying guarantee on what matters most: that the structure of the property holds up over the long term.

### The three building warranty periods

The Ley de Ordenación de la Edificación sets out three periods of liability according to the type of defect. It is the reference every buyer should be clear about.

| Period | Covers | Type of defect |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| 1 year | Finishes and completion | Defects in the execution of the finishing elements |
| 3 years | Habitability | Defects affecting habitability (damp, insulation, installations) |
| 10 years | Structure | Structural damage that compromises the stability of the building |

These guarantees sit alongside the protection of the deposit bond during construction, so that the buyer is protected both during the build and after handover.
