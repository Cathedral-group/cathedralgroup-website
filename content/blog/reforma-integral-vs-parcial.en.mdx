## Full renovation or partial renovation: the decision that defines your project

When a homeowner in Madrid sets out to improve their property, the first major decision is the scope of the works. The difference between a full renovation and a partial one is not simply a matter of budget: it is a strategic decision that affects the final result, the functionality of the home, its market value and, ultimately, the quality of life of those who live in it.

In this guide we offer a thorough comparison of the two options, with real price data for Madrid, project timelines, the advantages and drawbacks of each alternative and a practical decision framework that will help you determine which type of renovation best suits your needs and circumstances.

## Clear definitions: what each type of renovation includes

### Full renovation

A [full renovation](/servicios/reformas-integrales-madrid) involves the complete renewal of the home. Every system and finish in the property is reworked, stripping it back to a blank slate in order to rebuild from the structure up. It includes:

- **Complete demolition** of partition walls, wall coverings, flooring and existing suspended ceilings.
- **New layout** of the space according to the client's current needs.
- **Complete renewal of services**: electrics (new consumer unit, wiring and fittings), plumbing (new pipework, stopcocks and water points), drainage (soil stacks, waste pipes), telecommunications (fibre, data points).
- **Brand-new climate control**: ducted air conditioning, aerothermal systems, underfloor heating or a mixed system.
- **New wall coverings and flooring** throughout the home.
- **Complete interior joinery**: internal doors, fitted wardrobes, wardrobe fronts.
- **Exterior joinery**: new windows and balcony doors (thermal-break aluminium, PVC or timber).
- **Complete kitchen**: units, worktop, appliances and wall coverings.
- **Complete bathrooms**: sanitaryware, taps, wall coverings and bathroom furniture.
- **Final finishes**: paint, skirting boards, electrical fittings, lighting.

### Partial renovation

A partial renovation works on one or several specific rooms or systems without touching the home as a whole. The most common types are:

- **Bathroom renovation**: replacement of sanitaryware, taps, wall coverings and bathroom furniture. With or without changes to the layout.
- **Kitchen renovation**: new units, worktop, appliances and wall coverings. With or without a change of layout.
- **Bathroom + kitchen renovation**: the most sought-after combination in partial renovations.
- **Replacing flooring**: replacement of the flooring throughout the home or in selected rooms.
- **Upgrading services**: renewal of the electrical or plumbing installation without altering finishes (or with localised repairs).
- **Cosmetic refresh**: paint, new doors, updated lighting and fittings.

## Advantages and drawbacks of each option

### Full renovation

**Advantages:**

- **A coherent result**: the whole home follows a single approach to design, materials and quality. There are no jarring contrasts between renovated and un-renovated areas.
- **Optimising the layout**: the organisation of the space can be completely rethought to suit current needs (opening the kitchen onto the living room, creating a principal suite, improving storage, and so on).
- **Complete renewal of services**: the electrical and plumbing installations are replaced in full, which removes the risk of faults in ageing pipework or wiring and allows everything to be correctly sized.
- **Greater energy efficiency**: a whole-home approach makes it possible to introduce insulation, climate-control and joinery solutions that substantially improve the energy rating.
- **Greater uplift in property value**: a fully renovated home has a significantly higher market value than one that has only been partially renovated.
- **A single set of works**: everything is done at once, avoiding the inconvenience of having to carry out works in several phases over the years.

**Drawbacks:**

- **High investment**: the initial outlay is substantially greater.
- **Longer timeline**: between design, licences and construction, the complete process can stretch to between 6 and 12 months.
- **The need for alternative accommodation**: it is not possible to live in the home while the works are under way.
- **Greater administrative complexity**: it requires a works licence (minor or major) with a technical project and site management.
- **Greater risk of the unexpected**: working across the whole home increases the likelihood of encountering hidden problems (damp, the condition of the structure, deteriorated services).

### Partial renovation

**Advantages:**

- **Controlled investment**: it allows you to improve the most worn or highest-priority areas on a tighter budget.
- **Shorter timeline**: the works are completed in weeks rather than months.
- **You can carry on living in the home**: in most cases, with the disruption that comes with any building works.
- **Simpler paperwork**: many partial renovations require only prior notification, with no technical project or site management.
- **Lower risk of the unexpected**: limiting the scope reduces exposure to hidden problems.

**Drawbacks:**

- **An uneven result**: the renovated areas contrast with those left untouched, which can look aesthetically inconsistent.
- **Underlying problems go unresolved**: if the general services are old, renovating only the bathrooms and kitchen is a temporary fix.
- **Works may need to be repeated**: if in a few years you decide to renovate the rest of the home, some of the work already carried out may need altering or extending.
- **Smaller uplift in value**: the impact on market value is proportionally smaller than that of a full renovation.
- **Layout limitations**: a partial renovation cannot change the overall layout of the home, only that of the rooms affected.

## Decision framework: which do you need?

To help you determine which type of renovation suits your situation, we have developed a framework based on five key factors:

### Factor 1: Age of the services

| Condition of the services | Recommendation |
|----------------------------|---------------|
| Less than 15 years old, in good general condition | Partial renovation viable |
| 15-30 years old, working correctly but obsolete | Consider a full renovation |
| More than 30 years old or with recurring faults | Full renovation recommended |
| Electrical installation without an earth connection or with aluminium wiring | Full renovation necessary |

Old services are not only less efficient but can also pose a safety risk. An electrical installation more than 30 years old does not comply with current regulations (REBT) and may not cope with the demand of modern appliances.

### Factor 2: Current layout

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| The layout works well and only the finishes need updating | Partial renovation |
| Minor changes are needed (opening up the kitchen, combining rooms) | Full renovation advisable |
| The layout does not suit your lifestyle at all | Full renovation essential |

### Factor 3: How long you will stay in the home

| Expectation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Temporary home (less than 5 years) | Partial renovation |
| Medium-term home (5-15 years) | Depends on the general condition |
| Permanent home (more than 15 years) | Full renovation |
| Home for letting | Standard-level full renovation |
| Home for sale in the short term | Strategic partial renovation |

### Factor 4: Budget available

| Financial scope | Recommendation |
|--------------------------|---------------|
| Tight budget, no possibility of financing | Prioritised partial renovation |
| Moderate budget with financing available | Consider a standard full renovation |
| Comfortable budget | Full renovation to the desired level |

One important point: financing a full renovation through a renovation mortgage or a personal loan is usually more cost-effective than carrying out successive partial renovations, since the total accumulated cost of several partial renovations habitually exceeds that of a full renovation done in one go.

### Factor 5: The state of the property market

If the aim of the renovation is to increase the property's value for a future sale, the decision should take account of the return on investment (ROI):

| Type of renovation | Estimated ROI on sale |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Partial renovation (bathrooms + kitchen) | 60-80% of the investment |
| Standard full renovation | 70-90% of the investment |
| Premium full renovation (in a premium area) | 80-120% of the investment |
| Cosmetic partial renovation (paint + flooring) | 100-150% of the investment |

A cosmetic partial renovation (paint, flooring, lighting) offers the best ROI for a quick sale, as it radically transforms the visual appearance for a modest investment. That said, discerning buyers in premium areas will always prefer a full renovation.

## Common mistakes in each type of renovation

### Mistakes in partial renovations

**Renovating bathrooms and kitchen without checking the general services.** If the building's supply pipes or soil stacks are deteriorated, the bathroom renovation may be compromised in the medium term by problems that fall outside the scope of the works.

**Failing to consider how the look ties in with the rest of the home.** An ultra-modern bathroom in a home where the rest of the finishes date from the 1980s creates a contrast that detracts from the whole. If you opt for a partial renovation, take care that the materials and style you choose speak to the rest of the home.

**Underestimating the cost of successive renovations.** Doing the bathrooms first, then the kitchen, then the flooring and finally the painting means bringing in works teams four times over, with the associated costs of management, cleaning and disruption. The sum of these partial works can match or exceed the cost of having done it all at once.

### Mistakes in full renovations

**Not clearly defining the scope before starting.** A full renovation without a defined project and a fixed-price quote is a recipe for cost overruns. Every decision made on the fly tends to be more expensive than if it had been planned from the outset.

**Choosing the contractor on price alone.** In a full renovation, the quality of the work is decisive. A 15% saving on the initial quote can translate into a 30% cost overrun through defects, delays and reworking.

**Failing to set aside a contingency fund.** In full renovations it is common to encounter the unexpected: communal pipework in poor condition, beams with structural problems, hidden damp. Reserving 10-15% of the budget for contingencies is an indispensable measure of prudence.

**Neglecting site management.** Technical oversight of the works by a qualified professional is what ensures the work is carried out in line with the project and the regulations. Doing without this service to save money is a false economy that can have serious consequences.

## The middle ground: the strategic partial renovation

There is a third route worth considering: the strategic partial renovation. It involves working on the highest-impact areas (bathrooms, kitchen and shared spaces) while simultaneously renewing the home's general services (full electrics and plumbing), but without altering the layout or renewing the finishes in the bedrooms.

This option offers an appealing balance:

- The underlying problems are resolved (obsolete services).
- The most used and most visually impactful rooms are transformed.
- The budget is 40-60% lower than that of a complete full renovation.
- The construction timeline is significantly reduced.
- It establishes a solid foundation for completing the renovation of the bedrooms at a later stage if desired.

**Indicative cost for 100 m² with a high finish level**: 80.000 - 130.000 €.
