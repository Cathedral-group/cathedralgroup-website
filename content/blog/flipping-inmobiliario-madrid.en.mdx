## Property flipping in Madrid: buying, renovating and selling with judgement

Property flipping involves buying a home below its market value, renovating it to increase its worth and selling it within a short timeframe for a margin. In a market with the demand and liquidity of Madrid, it is one of the most popular [property investment](/capital) strategies among those looking to turn over capital within a defined time horizon. Done properly, flipping does not depend on the market "going up": the profit is generated at the point of purchase and in the quality of the renovation, not in speculation.

This guide explains how the process works, what figures to use as a reference and, above all, where the risks lie that separate a profitable deal from one that eats into the margin.

## What flipping is and why Madrid

Unlike buy-to-let (where the return comes in month by month), flipping seeks a capital gain on a single transaction. The investor takes on the acquisition cost, the renovation, the taxes and the holding costs over a few months, and recovers it all plus a profit at the point of sale.

Madrid brings together the conditions this strategy needs:

- **Demand-side liquidity**: a renovated and well-presented property finds a buyer quickly.
- **Scope for capital appreciation**: there is a stock of older or un-modernised housing with clear room for improvement.
- **A finish differential**: in high-end areas, a quality renovation feeds directly into the sale price.

According to market indices, in flipping deals executed with discipline the **typical margin sits between 20% and 40%** on the investment, and a renovated and properly marketed property **sells on average in around 60 days**. These are indicative ranges: they depend on the area, the purchase price and the execution.

## The 70% rule: the purchase filter

The most widely used tool for deciding how much to pay is the **70% rule**. Its logic is straightforward: to leave room for profit, taxes and unforeseen costs, the purchase price should not exceed 70% of the estimated post-renovation value, less the cost of that renovation.

> **Maximum purchase price = (70% of the post-renovation value) − cost of the renovation**

The post-renovation value (known as the ARV, or *After Repair Value*) is the realistic price at which the property can be sold once finished, estimated from genuine comparables of the same area and type.

### Worked example using the 70% rule

| Item | Reference amount |
|----------|----------------------|
| Estimated post-renovation value (ARV) | 500.000 € |
| 70% of the ARV | 350.000 € |
| Estimated cost of the renovation | 90.000 € |
| **Recommended maximum purchase price** | **260.000 €** |

The remaining 30% is not clean profit: it is the buffer that absorbs the ITP (transfer tax) on the purchase, notary and registry fees, marketing fees, the months of holding costs (IBI council tax, service charges, utilities) and unforeseen building costs. The profit comes out of that margin once everything above is covered. This is why **buying above the price set by the rule is the costliest mistake in flipping**: it erodes the margin before you even begin.

## The process step by step

A well-run flipping deal always follows the same sequence:

1. **Search and analysis**: identify properties priced below the market and estimate the ARV using reliable comparables. You can draw on our analysis of [where to invest in Madrid in 2026](/blog/donde-invertir-madrid-2026-rentabilidad) to narrow down areas with room to grow.
2. **Due diligence**: check for charges, the registry position, the state of the building's residents' association, possible special levies and the technical feasibility of the planned renovation.
3. **Purchase**: close at a price consistent with the 70% rule, leaving a genuine margin.
4. **Renovation**: carry out a [full renovation](/servicios/reformas-integrales-madrid) that maximises the perceived value and keeps to the planned timeframe and budget.
5. **Marketing and sale**: present the property (with *home staging* where appropriate) and sell it within the ~60-day window indicated by market indices.

The critical point is stage 4. The renovation is where value is created, but it is also where cost overruns and delays are concentrated. Every extra month the works drag on is another month of taxes, financing and holding costs eating into the margin.

## The ITP tax benefit in Madrid

The Region of Madrid offers a tax incentive designed precisely for this activity. The transfer tax (ITP, *Impuesto sobre Transmisiones Patrimoniales*), which applies as standard to the purchase of a second-hand home, can benefit from a **reduced rate of 2% instead of 6%** when the property is acquired by a professional for resale, provided the **sale is completed within three years** of the purchase.

| Item | Standard rate | Reduced resale rate |
|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| ITP on the purchase of a second-hand home | 6% | 2% |
| Condition | — | Buy, renovate and resell within 3 years |

On a purchase of 260.000 €, that four-point difference represents a reference saving of around 10.400 € on the acquisition tax, which goes straight into the deal's margin. It is a meaningful incentive, but **it is subject to strict formal requirements**: the buyer's status, meeting the deadline and the documentation required by regional legislation. The precise fit must be reviewed case by case with tax advice, in accordance with the legislation in force in the Region of Madrid (published in the BOCM, the regional official gazette) and the criteria of the AEAT (Spanish tax authority). It is worth confirming the applicable conditions at the time of each deal, as regional incentives may be updated.

## The risks: where the margin is lost

Flipping is not a risk-free strategy. These are the fronts that spoil the most deals:

- **Overestimating the ARV**: if the projected sale price is optimistic, the whole calculation falls apart. The ARV should be based on comparables that have sold, not on listings.
- **Building cost overruns**: especially in older buildings, where the services or the structure can hold surprises. The contingency buffer is not optional.
- **Timeframes that stretch**: each extra month of works adds holding and financing costs, and can put the three-year window for the ITP benefit at risk.
- **Cost of financing**: if the deal is leveraged, the interest runs while the capital is tied up.
- **Slow marketing**: the reference sale timeframe (~60 days) is met when the product and the price are right; a pricing error can leave the property stuck.

Most of these risks are managed with two levers: **a disciplined purchase analysis** (the 70% rule and a realistic ARV) and **genuine control over the renovation** in terms of timeframe, cost and quality.
