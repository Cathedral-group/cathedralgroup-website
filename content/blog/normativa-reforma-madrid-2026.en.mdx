## Renovation regulations in Madrid 2026: the legal framework every property owner should know

Undertaking a renovation in Madrid without understanding the regulatory framework in force is a risk that can translate into financial penalties, work being halted and even an obligation to undo what has already been done. The planning and building regulations in the capital are extensive and complex, but a sound grasp of them is essential to ensure that any intervention is carried out within the law.

This guide brings together all the relevant regulations for renovations in Madrid, updated for 2026, from the Código Técnico de la Edificación (Technical Building Code) to the specific municipal ordinances, taking in the requirements of residents' associations and the mandatory certificates along the way.

### The Código Técnico de la Edificación (CTE): the regulatory foundation

The CTE (Technical Building Code) is the national regulatory framework that sets out the basic quality requirements that buildings in Spain must meet. Although it applies more stringently to new builds, many of its requirements are also mandatory in renovations, particularly where these affect structural elements or the building envelope.

**Basic Document on Structural Safety (DB-SE)**

Any renovation that alters the building's load-bearing structure (opening up apertures in load-bearing walls, removing columns, reinforcing floor slabs) must be supported by a project drawn up by an architect guaranteeing compliance with the DB-SE. This includes:

- Structural calculations for both the current and the renovated state.
- Verification of the floor slab's load-bearing capacity for new uses (for example, the installation of a natural stone bath that can weigh more than 500 kg).
- Approval by the Madrid College of Architects (COAM).

**Basic Document on Fire Safety (DB-SI)**

Renovations that involve a change of use or a significant alteration of the layout must comply with the fire protection requirements. This applies in particular to:

- Changes of use from commercial premises to a home, where the requirements for fire compartmentation, evacuation and detection are strict. See our dedicated guide on [changing the use of commercial premises to a home](/servicios/cambio-uso-local-vivienda-madrid).
- The installation of fireplaces or wood-burning stoves, which require a flue for extracting smoke that complies with the regulations.
- Wall and ceiling finishes on evacuation routes, which must have a specified reaction-to-fire classification.

**Basic Document on Health and Hygiene (DB-HS)**

This governs the conditions for ventilation, drainage and indoor air quality. It is especially relevant in bathroom and kitchen renovations:

- Mandatory minimum ventilation in bathrooms, kitchens and bedrooms.
- Mechanical extraction systems where there is no direct natural ventilation.
- Waterproofing of roofs and façades when work is carried out on the building envelope.

**Basic Document on Energy Saving (DB-HE)**

Renovations affecting more than 25% of the building envelope must comply with the updated energy efficiency requirements. This includes:

- Minimum thermal insulation in façades, roofs and floors.
- Efficiency of heating and cooling installations.
- A minimum contribution from renewable energy in certain cases.

**Basic Document on Noise Protection (DB-HR)**

This sets out the maximum levels of impact and airborne noise between homes. It is relevant when interior partition walls or floors are altered, since the new construction system must guarantee acoustic insulation equal to or better than what was there before.

### Madrid City Council planning regulations

**General Urban Development Plan (PGOU)**

Madrid's PGOU governs which interventions are possible according to the planning classification of the property. Key considerations:

- **Buildable area:** The constructed floor area cannot be increased without an extension licence that complies with the planning parameters.
- **Permitted uses:** Each zone has a set of authorised uses. A home cannot be converted into an office, or vice versa, without a formal change of use.
- **Heights and volumes:** Renovations that alter the external massing (enclosing terraces, raising penthouses) require planning permission.
- **Heritage protection:** Listed buildings are subject to additional restrictions on façades, roofs and communal elements.

**Types of licence according to the type of works**

As we set out in our [guide to building permits in Madrid](/blog/licencia-obra-madrid-guia), there are different types of authorisation depending on the scale of the works:

- **Declaración responsable (self-certified works declaration):** For minor works that affect neither the structure nor the façade. This covers bathroom and kitchen renovations without altering partition walls, replacing floors, interior painting and renewing installations within the home. Processing time: immediate (work can begin as soon as it is submitted).

- **Minor works licence (licencia de obra menor):** For interventions that alter the internal layout without affecting the structure. This covers removing or building non-load-bearing partitions, opening up apertures in internal divisions and modifying communal installations. Processing time: 1-3 months.

- **Major works licence (licencia de obra mayor):** For renovations that affect the structure, the façade or the building's massing. This requires a technical project approved by the COAM. Processing time: 3-6 months or more.

### Ordinance on protection against noise pollution

Madrid has one of the strictest sets of noise regulations in Spain, and breaching them can lead to penalties of up to 300,000 euros in the most serious cases. The key points for renovations are:

**Permitted working hours**

- **Weekdays:** from 8:00 to 21:00.
- **Saturdays:** from 9:30 to 21:00.
- **Sundays and public holidays:** noise-generating works are prohibited.

These are the general hours, but many residents' associations set additional restrictions in their by-laws (for example, banning works in August or reducing the hours to 9:00-19:00).

**Maximum noise levels**

The ordinance sets sound emission limits that works must respect. In practice, the noisiest tasks (demolition, drilling, cutting materials) should preferably be carried out during the middle of the day and never first thing in the morning or last thing in the afternoon.

**Notifying neighbours**

Although it is not legally mandatory in every case, good practice (and many residents' association rules) requires neighbours to be notified of the start of the works, their estimated duration and the planned hours for the noisiest tasks.

### Technical Building Inspection (ITE)

The ITE is a mandatory inspection that buildings in Madrid more than 30 years old must pass. Its relevance to renovations is twofold:

**An obligation prior to the renovation**

If the building is due to pass its ITE and has not done so, the authorities can refuse the building permit. It is essential to check the status of the building's ITE before starting any application.

**What the inspection covers**

The ITE assesses:

- The condition of the load-bearing structure (foundations, walls, columns, floor slabs).
- The condition of the façades (finishes, cladding, ornamental elements).
- The condition of roofs and flat roofs.
- The communal drainage, plumbing and electrical installations.
- The building's accessibility provisions.

**Ratings and consequences**

- **Favourable:** The building meets the minimum standards.
- **Favourable with minor deficiencies:** Requires minor works within set timeframes.
- **Unfavourable:** Requires urgent or very urgent works that may affect the viability or the schedule of an interior renovation.

**Building Evaluation Report (IEE)**

Since the regulatory update, the IEE has broadened the scope of the ITE to include an assessment of the building's energy efficiency and its accessibility provisions, in addition to its state of repair.

### Accessibility requirements

Accessibility regulations have come to the fore in recent years, and renovations are not exempt from these requirements:

**In communal elements**

Renovations affecting the building's communal areas (entrance hall, stairs, lift) must improve accessibility provisions where this is technically and economically viable. The installation of a lift, the removal of steps at the entrance or adjusting the width of doors may all be required.

**In private homes**

Although the regulations do not require private homes to be accessible, universal design standards are increasingly applied as a marker of quality:

- Doors with a minimum width of 80 cm (82-85 cm recommended).
- Level-access showers with no step.
- Switches at an accessible height (between 80 and 120 cm from the floor).
- Sufficient turning space in bathrooms and kitchens.

**Obligations on the residents' association**

The Horizontal Property Act requires residents' associations to carry out the necessary accessibility works when requested by an owner or resident who has a disability or is over 70, provided the cost does not exceed twelve ordinary monthly instalments of communal charges (or that public grants are available to cover 75% of the cost).

### Energy efficiency certificate

**When it is mandatory**

The energy efficiency certificate (CEE) is mandatory for selling or letting a home. In the context of a renovation, its relevance is as follows:

- If the renovation aims to improve energy efficiency, a CEE before and after allows the improvement to be documented and grants to be accessed.
- Renovations that affect the thermal envelope or the heating and cooling installations must achieve an energy rating equal to or better than the previous one.
- The Next Generation European funds and the grants under the Region of Madrid's Renovation Plan require a jump of at least one band in the energy rating in order to qualify for the subsidies.

**How to improve the rating**

The most effective interventions for improving the energy rating are:

- Thermal insulation of the façade (from the inside if it is not possible from the outside).
- Replacing windows with thermally broken frames and low-emissivity glazing.
- Installing efficient heating and cooling systems (aerothermal energy, underfloor heating).
- Adding renewable energy (solar thermal or photovoltaic panels).

### Regulations on installations

**Low-voltage electrical regulations (REBT)**

Any renovation that alters the electrical installation must comply with the REBT (low-voltage electrical regulations) in force. The main requirements are:

- Minimum level of electrification: high (9,200 W) for homes larger than 160 m² or with electric heating, air conditioning, automation or electric vehicle charging.
- Mandatory dedicated circuits for lighting, general sockets, kitchen, washing machine/dishwasher, bathroom and heating.
- High-sensitivity residual current devices (30 mA) on all circuits.
- An electrical compliance certificate signed by an authorised installer on completion of the works.

**Regulation on Thermal Installations in Buildings (RITE)**

This governs heating, cooling and domestic hot water installations. It is relevant when air conditioning and heating systems are installed or modified:

- A minimum energy efficiency for the equipment.
- A requirement for periodic maintenance for installations of more than 70 kW.
- Ventilation requirements associated with heating and cooling systems.

**Gas installations**

Altering gas installations (relocating a boiler, moving a gas hob) requires the involvement of an authorised installer and the issue of an installation certificate. The current trend is towards progressive electrification, replacing gas boilers with aerothermal systems.

### Residents' association: required approvals

Dealing with the residents' association is a critical aspect of any renovation. The approval requirements vary according to the scope of the works:

**Works that do not require approval from the association**

- Interior renovations that affect neither communal elements nor the structure.
- Replacing floors, painting, renovating bathrooms and kitchens (without altering communal drainpipes).
- Installing air conditioning where internal rules permit it.

**Works that require notification to the association**

- Any works that generate noise, the movement of materials through communal areas or the placing of skips on the public highway.
- Modifications to installations that connect to the communal ones (drainpipes, water risers).

**Works that require approval by a simple majority**

- Installing air conditioning units on the façade (where no prior rules exist).
- Modifying non-structural communal elements.

**Works that require approval by a qualified majority (3/5)**

- Modifications affecting the building's façade.
- Works on communal elements that constitute a non-mandatory improvement.

**Works that require unanimity**

- Structural modifications to the building.
- Altering the ownership shares.
- Changes to the association's founding deed.

### Penalties for non-compliance

Failing to comply with the regulations can have serious consequences:

- **Works without a licence:** Fines of 600 to 6,000 € for minor works and of 6,001 to 600,000 € for major works carried out without the corresponding licence.
- **Breach of working hours:** Fines of 750 to 3,000 € for infringing the noise ordinance.
- **Improper waste disposal:** Penalties of up to 30,000 € for depositing rubble outside the authorised skips.
- **Demolition order:** In the most serious cases, the authorities can order the property to be restored to its original state at the owner's expense.

### Grants and assistance available in 2026

There are various funding streams that can partly finance a renovation:

- **Region of Madrid home renovation plan:** Grants of up to 40% of the cost of energy efficiency and accessibility improvements.
- **Next Generation funds (periodic calls for applications):** Grants of up to 21,400 € per home for comprehensive energy renovation.
- **Income tax (IRPF) deductions:** Deductions of up to 60% of the amounts invested in energy renovation works, up to a maximum of 15,000 €.
- **Reduced VAT:** Renovations on homes more than 2 years old are taxed at 10% VAT (instead of 21%) where the cost of materials does not exceed 40% of the total invoice.

### Practical recommendations

**Engage qualified professionals**

The complexity of the regulations makes it essential to have an architect or technical architect who knows the Madrid regulations and can process the necessary licences. The apparent saving from doing without a qualified professional can translate into legal and financial problems far greater than their fees.

**Document everything in writing**

Keep copies of licences, communications with the association, installation certificates and certificates of completion. This documentation will be needed for future transactions involving the property and in the event of any claim.

**Plan for administrative timeframes**

Licence procedures can delay the renovation by several months. Building these timeframes into the project schedule avoids frustration and the additional costs caused by delays.
